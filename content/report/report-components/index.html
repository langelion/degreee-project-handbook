    Report components¶

    Below are explained the fundamental components that are common to every report.

    Cover and title page¶

    Before submitting the final report it should be wrapped by the official KTH thesis cover to give it a uniform cover (back and front). The cover is generated using this online form.

    It is recommended to maintain the title page of the report template in order to indicate the examiner and the supervisor(s) which do not show up on the KTH cover page.

    Abstract¶

    The abstract summarizes the report. It does so by being a condensed version of the report's separate sections: Background, Methods, Results, and Conclusion.

    Background¶

    • Provide the reader with a background to the study in a couple of sentences.
    • Explain what is already known and what is not known about the subject.

    Methods¶

    • Provide enough information to enable the reader to understand what was done and how.

    • Which method and evaluation measures were used?

    Results¶

    This is the most important part of the abstract since it contains the findings of the study. It should therefore be the longest part of the abstract.

    Conclusions¶

    Finally, write down the most important take-home message of the study. It can also include a short mentioning of the implications of the study.

    Reference: Indian journal of psychiatry

    Remember: The abstract should be written in both English and Swedish. Avoid to make the Swedish version a literal translation of the English one, but try to write it in a natural language.

    Tip

    Write the abstracts as the last component of the report. It has dependencies on all other core sections.

    Table of contents¶

    The skeleton of a scientific report is its structure which is composed of main sections and subsections. They should be organized in a logical order to help reinforce the report's scientific contribution and story line.

    It is important to have a balanced section structure. Only introduce subsections if the main section (X) will contain at least two of subsections (e.g. X.1 and X.2).

    
1 Introduction
  1.1 Topic 1 (remove!)
2 Background
  2.1 Topic 1
  2.2 Topic 2
  2.3 Topic 3

    For the table of contents, it is highly recommended to

    • auto-generate the table of contents in the text editor (don't write it manually),

    • use the section titles to reinforce the report's message and reveal the story line. For example, repeat key points (typically from the title and abstract) in the section titles,

    • make it easy to navigate between different parts of the report (add hyperlinks),

    • assure that the structure is informative and concise (avoid being too detailed or too condensed)

    Bibliography¶

    The bibliography is the final section of the report and includes a complete list of sources that were used to develop the research project. Complete citations (including the author's name, the title, publisher, year pubilshed, page numbers, URLs) should be listed for any in-text citation in the report. The latter should provide a brief idea where the information to support a specific statement in the report can be found.

    There exist many different bibliographic styles for referencing and citing literature. Popular styles for scientific reports include IEEE, ACM, Harvard, Chicago, APA, etc. The most important is not which style to use, but to be consequent and stick to only one throughout the report. This is easily achieved by letting the reference manager and/or text editor (e.g. BibTeX) handle the generation of cited sources according to the selected style.

    • ACM, APA: Lists references alphabetically, using the author's last name.
    • IEEE: Lists references numerically, in the order they have been cited.

    Notice the difference in citing a journal article, internet resource, and book.
